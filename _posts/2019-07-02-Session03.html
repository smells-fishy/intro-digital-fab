Class (July 2)

During this class, we explored Fusion 360, a Computer Assisted Design (CAD) software. CAD is really powerful because it allows us to visualize the desired outcome and enables machining using precise machines.

Designs in Fusion are based off of two-dimensional sketches, which can be extruded into three-dimensional bodies. Fusion allows us to assemble the final design in the program and to test the fit of the pieces in the program before using any materials. Some example sketches can be downloaded here.

The sketches in Fusion can be used as models for two-dimensional machining using the laser cutter. The laser cutter is a powerful and precise tool that can burn through many types of material. You can cut many types of materials, such as wood, cardboard, and acrylic. However, one must not cut polycarbonate and polyvinyl chloride, due to the release of toxic fumes.

The laser cutter can be used to machine press-fit pieces from cardboard. Press-fit pieces are components fitted together using friction as the joining force.

My next project will be to create a press-fit box.

Two Labs (July 3 and July 8)

For these two labs, I tried to make a press-fit box and a prototype using cardboard and a laser cutter. First, I created some CAD files for the box and a final project prototype, which are available for download here. My first box was just a test, but the second one was designed to hold cards from Magic: The Gathering. For these projects, I used carboard of a thickness of 4.6 mm.

Initially, I made a test box to analyze the kerf of the laser cutter. I chose some arbitrary dimensions (all multiples of 10 for ease of calculation) for the box, created a CAD file, and then cut the pieces out. The kerf was found to be roughly 0.4mm through measurement by calipers. I found that in order to make proper tabs and notches in the box faces, you must add kerf to the tab dimensions, and subtract kerf from the notch dimensions. This should result in proper dimensions and a tight fit between the pieces. However, my box did not result in a tight fit, and the individual pieces are shown below. Furthermore, I forgot to account for the thickness of the cardboard in my dimensions, which resulted in the box ends fitting improperly.

I moved on to making my box to store Magic cards. Magic cards are 2.5 in by 3.5 in, so roughly 63.5 mm by 88.9 mm. In order to allow for a looser fit, I made the dimensions 63.8 mm by 89 mm. Accounting for the kerf, this became 63.4 mm by 88.6 mm. Then, since the notches were on the vertical faces, the vertical dimension became 97.8 mm in order to accomodate for the thickness of the cardboard, rounded up to 98 mm to allow for a looser fit.

After developing the CAD file, I proceeded to cut the box out. Although the fit was not tight, the box held together quite nicely without support.

In order to give the box the extra support that it needed, I used hot glue on the tabs to join the pieces (cheating!) and to hold them in place. Unfortunately, due to the lack of special connector pieces, even if the box were a tight fit it would be prone to falling apart.

Next, I decided to pursue a cardboard prototype of my final project idea. I created a motor bracing with the cardboard, and created a piece to connect the braces and act as a base. Measurements of the motor indicated that it was roughly 33 mm long, had a case diameter of 27.4 mm, and a shaft diameter of 2.3 mm.

After cutting, I found that one of the pieces was improperly dimensioned because I didn't account for the thickness of the cardboard. Unfortunately, I ran out of time during lab, so the project will have to be picked up next time. In the meantime, the CAD file will be fixed and redimensioned.
